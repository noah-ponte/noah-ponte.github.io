Shutter Speed

The length of time at which the media is exposed to light

Analogue cameras that utilize film have to keep that film unexposed to light until it is time to take an image

Digital cameras utilize an image sensor to fast-track the process of film by taking and processing the image all at once

A factor to consider with shutter speed is motion blur. This allows motion to be condensed into an image by capturing a length of time. An example from the home page is a car's headlights traveling along a highway.
With a long shutter speed we see the length of the car's travel and not just an instant by using a longer shutter speed.

Common shutter speeds are listed below:

  • 1/4000s: The fastest shutter speed on a digital Sony a6000 camera
  • 1/1000s: The fastest shutter speed on a 35mm analogue Canon AE-1 camera & on a large format Graflex Speed Graphic camera
  • 1/60s: Objectively the slowest shutter speed with minimal motion blur
  • 1/10s: The slowest shutter speed on a large format Graflex Speed Graphic camera
  • 2s: The slowest shutter speed on a 35mm analogue Canon AE-1 camera
  • 30s: The slowest shutter speed on a digital Sony a6000 camera
  • Bulb: This shutter speed allows the user to control how long the media is exposed to light for. Some cameras require the shutter button to be held for the entire length
